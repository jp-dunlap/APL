Definitive Report: Legislative Complicity

Act 710: An Anatomy of the Lawfare Apparatus

Arkansas Act 710 of 2017 is the central pillar of a sophisticated, multi-layered "lawfare" apparatus designed to enforce ideological conformity and shield the Israeli state from accountability. This definitive intelligence dossier provides an exhaustive analysis of the law's ideological origins, its weaponization against constitutionally protected speech, its central role in a coordinated national campaign of political repression, and its devastating impact on academic and public discourse within the state.

I. The National Context & The "Model Bill" Strategy

Act 710 is not an isolated or organic piece of Arkansas legislation. It is a local franchise of a coordinated, national political project. The law is a quintessential example of a "model bill" or "cookie-cutter legislation," a tactic where a centrally-developed legislative template is distributed to and adopted by state legislatures across the country to advance a specific agenda. This strategy, perfected by right-wing groups like the American Legislative Exchange Council (ALEC), was deployed with ruthless efficiency by the pro-Israel lobby to combat the growing influence of the Boycott, Divestment, Sanctions (BDS) movement.

The BDS movement, formally launched in 2005 by Palestinian civil society, was inspired by the successful international campaign against South African apartheid. Its call for non-violent economic pressure on entities complicit in the occupation of Palestine gained significant traction globally, prompting a massive political backlash. Between 2015 and 2019, at least twenty-seven states, including Arkansas, passed anti-BDS laws, many of them nearly identical in language and structure. This demonstrates a centrally-directed campaign to create a unified legal front against Palestine solidarity activism.

II. Legislative Genesis: A Fusion of Ideology and Politics

The passage of Act 710, introduced as Senate Bill 513, was driven by a direct fusion of Christian Zionist eschatology with the raw political power of the Republican supermajority in the Arkansas General Assembly. The public statements of the bill's sponsors provide irrefutable proof that the law's primary intent was theological and political, a fact that stands in stark contradiction to the state's later legal defense of the law as a purely commercial regulation.

The Sponsors' Confession: An Eschatological Mandate

The primary sponsor, Senator Bart Hester, articulated the law's purpose not in terms of economics, but as a fulfillment of his religious duty, rooted in an End Times theology that views the modern state of Israel as a necessary precursor to the Second Coming of Christ.

"There is going to be certain things that happen in Israel before Christ returns. There will be famines and disease and war. And the Jewish people are going to go back to their homeland. At that point Jesus Christ will come back to the earth. Anybody, Jewish or not Jewish, that doesn't accept Christ, in my opinion, will end up going to hell."

- Senator Bart Hester, Primary Sponsor of Act 710, as quoted in the documentary "Boycott"

The bill's House sponsor, Representative Jim Dotson, was equally explicit about the law's political intent, stating that it was a direct reaction to the BDS movement and a signal that Arkansas would "not going to support any entity that is trying to cause detriment to the state of Israel." These public declarations expose the law's true nature as a tool of ideological warfare, designed to punish political dissent and enforce support for a foreign state based on a specific religious worldview.

III. The Legal Challenge: Arkansas Times LP v. Waldrip

The constitutionality of Act 710 was immediately challenged in a landmark case that became a national focal point for the struggle over free speech and Palestine solidarity. The lawsuit was filed by the Arkansas Times, a Little Rock-based newsmagazine, after it was informed by the University of Arkansas-Pulaski Technical College that it would have to sign the anti-boycott pledge to continue its long-standing advertising contract.

The Core Legal Arguments

The Plaintiff's Position (ACLU / Arkansas Times): The lawsuit argued that Act 710 imposes an unconstitutional condition on public contracts. It forces individuals and companies to surrender their First Amendment right to participate in politically motivated boycotts in order to do business with the state. This argument was grounded in the powerful Supreme Court precedent of NAACP v. Claiborne Hardware Co. (1982), which affirmed that non-violent political boycotts are a form of protected speech.

The State's Defense: The State of Arkansas argued that the law does not regulate speech but rather "purely commercial, non-expressive conduct." It claimed that the act of boycotting—the purchasing decision itself—is invisible and not inherently expressive, and therefore does not receive First Amendment protection.

The Judicial Journey: A Victory Annulled

The case followed a tortuous path through the federal courts:

  • January 2019: A federal district court dismisses the lawsuit.
  • February 2021: In a major victory for free speech advocates, a three-judge panel of the Eighth Circuit Court of Appeals reverses the lower court, ruling that the law "seeks to restrict government contractors' ability to participate in speech and other protected, boycott-associated activities."
  • June 2022: In a deeply controversial move, the full Eighth Circuit Court of Appeals, sitting en banc, vacates the panel's decision and upholds Act 710. The majority opinion adopted the state's argument, stating the law "only prohibits economic decisions that discriminate against Israel."
  • February 2023: The U.S. Supreme Court declines to hear the case, allowing the repressive Eighth Circuit ruling to stand.

The final judicial outcome effectively carves out a "Palestine Exception to Free Speech," legitimizing the state's power to use its financial leverage to enforce ideological conformity on its contractors.

IV. The Chilling Effect: A Weapon Against Dissent

The legal validation of Act 710 has had a direct, tangible, and devastating chilling effect on academic freedom and public discourse in Arkansas. The law has been actively weaponized to silence and financially penalize individuals critical of Israeli state policy, creating an atmosphere of fear and self-censorship, particularly within the state's public university system.

Case File: The Suppression of Nathan Thrall

In October 2023, the University of Arkansas, Fayetteville, invited Nathan Thrall, a highly respected Jewish American journalist and author, to speak about his new book, "A Day in the Life of Abed Salama." The university was forced to inform Thrall that he could not be paid his speaker's fee unless he first signed the state-mandated pledge not to boycott Israel. Thrall refused.

The incident was amplified by Governor Sarah Huckabee Sanders, who praised the university's enforcement of the law on social media, stating: "On campuses across America, pro-Hamas protesters are harassing Jews, praising terrorism, and calling for the destruction of Israel. That is unacceptable and won't happen in Arkansas." This statement deliberately and falsely conflated Thrall's nuanced, humanistic work with terrorism, demonstrating how the law provides political cover for the suppression of any viewpoint that challenges the dominant pro-Israel narrative.

Case File: The Penalization of Dr. Steve Feldman

In February 2023, Dr. Steve Feldman, a Jewish American dermatologist with family in Israel, delivered a virtual grand rounds presentation to the University of Arkansas for Medical Sciences (UAMS). When presented with the anti-boycott certification required for his \$500 honorarium, he refused to sign. Dr. Feldman, who founded the Jewish Museum of the Palestinian Experience, cited his moral and religious objections to Israel's treatment of Palestinians as the reason for his refusal. As a result, UAMS denied his payment.

"I support boycotts for ending the mistreatment of the Palestinian people," Dr. Feldman stated. "Here's a Jewish American doctor with family in Israel and he won't sign it!"

This case powerfully illustrates the absurdity and cruelty of the law, forcing a Jewish American to choose between his conscience and his compensation, and weaponizing the state's financial power to enforce a political position he finds morally repugnant.

V. The Legacy of Lawfare: A Replicable Model of Repression

The successful implementation and legal defense of Act 710 has created a dangerous and replicable template for corporate and right-wing interests to suppress any form of boycott-based activism. The law's framework has been explicitly copied to protect other favored industries from public accountability.

From Anti-BDS to Anti-ESG

In 2023, the Arkansas legislature passed new laws explicitly modeled on Act 710 that prohibit public entities from contracting with companies that "boycott" the fossil fuel or firearms industries. This demonstrates that the ultimate goal of this lawfare strategy extends far beyond the issue of Palestine; it is a broad-based assault on the very concept of corporate accountability and ethical consumption, designed to insulate politically powerful industries from non-violent economic pressure.

Furthermore, Arkansas Attorney General Tim Griffin has leveraged Act 710's precedent on the national stage, leading a coalition of 24 state attorneys general to threaten Brown University with legal consequences if it proceeds with a proposal to divest from companies complicit in the occupation. This action confirms Act 710's role as not just a tool of in-state repression, but as a model and justification for a nationwide campaign to crush the student solidarity movement.
