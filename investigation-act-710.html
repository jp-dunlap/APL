Report: Legislative Complicity

Act 710: The Lawfare Apparatus

Arkansas Act 710 of 2017 is a key instrument of state-level complicity, designed to suppress Palestine solidarity activism by leveraging state contracts as a tool of political repression. This analysis deconstructs the law, its ideological underpinnings, and its role in a coordinated, national campaign to shield the Israeli state from accountability.

I. Legislative Genesis & Ideological Intent

Act 710 functions to codify state support for the Israeli apartheid regime by mandating that public entities refrain from contracting with companies boycotting Israel. The law’s stated justifications—combating "economic warfare" and "discrimination"—are a deliberate inversion of material reality. They mask the primary political objective: to suppress dissent against Israeli policies by chilling free speech and delegitimizing the grassroots, non-violent Boycott, Divestment, Sanctions (BDS) movement.

The Sponsors of Repression

The law was spearheaded by key Republican legislators whose motivations reveal a confluence of Christian Zionism and a political desire to protect Israeli state interests.

"I believe in the Bible, so I absolutely believe the Jewish people are God's chosen people and therefore I have a responsibility to benefit them where possible... we are not going to stand by in Arkansas and let people take our taxpayer money against something that... our taxpayers don't believe in."

- Senator Bart Hester, Primary Sponsor of Act 710

According to State Representative Jim Dotson, the Act was a reaction to the BDS Movement, showing that the State was "not going to support any entity that is trying to cause detriment to the state of Israel."

- Representative Jim Dotson, Primary Sponsor of Act 710

II. The Legal Challenge: Arkansas Times LP v. Waldrip

The law was challenged by the Arkansas Times newspaper, which refused to sign the anti-boycott pledge as a condition of receiving state advertising contracts. The case, supported by the ACLU, became a focal point for contesting the constitutionality of anti-BDS laws nationwide.

The Core Argument: A Violation of Free Speech

The central argument against Act 710 was that it imposes an unconstitutional condition on public contracts, forcing contractors to give up their First Amendment right to participate in politically motivated boycotts. Boycotts have long been recognized as a protected form of expressive conduct, most notably in the Supreme Court case NAACP v. Claiborne Hardware Co.

The Judicial Outcome: A Blow to Free Speech

After an initial victory for the Arkansas Times at a three-judge panel, the full 8th Circuit Court of Appeals reversed the decision and upheld Act 710. The court asserted that the "purchasing decisions at the heart of a boycott are not expressive enough to merit First Amendment protection." In February 2023, the U.S. Supreme Court declined to hear the case, leaving the repressive law in place.

This outcome effectively legitimizes the state's power to enforce ideological conformity on its contractors, sanctioning what has been termed the "Palestine Exception to Free Speech" and setting a dangerous precedent for similar laws across the country.
