Report: The Military-Industrial Complex

The Military-Police Nexus

Arkansas functions as a critical, yet often overlooked, nexus in the circulation of tools and tactics of state repression. A symbiotic relationship exists between the state's actively promoted defense-industrial complex—which exports weaponry to global conflict zones—and the observable militarization of its domestic law enforcement agencies, which import the tools and tactics of control.

I. Forging the Arsenal: Arkansas's State-Sanctioned Defense Industry

The aerospace and defense sector is actively cultivated by state government as a cornerstone of economic development, making Arkansas a key node in the U.S. defense supply chain and a direct participant in the global arms trade.

The Camden Defense Hub

Camden, in South Arkansas, hosts major operations for the world's largest arms dealers. Lockheed Martin produces the HIMARS rocket system and PAC-3 missiles. General Dynamics manufactures 155mm artillery projectiles. Aerojet Rocketdyne (L3Harris) produces solid rocket motors essential for a wide range of missiles. These facilities make Arkansas a direct exporter of the instruments of modern warfare.

The Export of Repressive Capacity

The products manufactured in Arkansas are fundamental instruments of state-imposed control. Systems like HIMARS, PAC-3 missiles, and GMLRS rockets are used in high-intensity military operations globally, including by the Israeli military. The marketing of these weapons as "combat-proven" links Arkansas's industrial output directly to the global apparatus of control and violent suppression.

II. Importing Repression: The Militarization of Arkansas Police

While Arkansas defense contractors export weapons, the state's domestic police forces import militarized tactics, equipment, and ideologies, completing a circuit where the methodologies of control are both disseminated from and adopted within the state.

Militarized Units and Equipment

Arkansas's primary law enforcement agencies have made significant investments in militarized equipment and specialized units. The Arkansas State Police (ASP) operates SWAT and Emergency Response Teams (ERT) trained in counterterrorism, explosive breaching, and crowd control, utilizing armored vehicles like the BEARCAT. Both ASP and the North Little Rock Police Department (NLRPD) operate drone units for surveillance and suspect tracking.

Tactics of Control

The practices of Arkansas law enforcement show parallels with repressive tactics used in military occupations. The ASP's state Fusion Center, part of a national DHS/FBI network, has the capacity for broad surveillance. The existence of militarized crowd control units (ASP's ERT) demonstrates a preparedness that could be deployed against public protests, chilling free speech. Persistent complaints of racial bias indicate ongoing community concerns about discriminatory policing, which can be exacerbated by a militarized posture.

III. The Symbiotic Loop

The state's vigorous promotion of its defense industry for economic benefit creates an environment that reinforces the militarization of its own police forces. This creates a cycle where the tools and logics of control are both exported globally and reflected domestically.

Economic Imperatives and Political Cover

The significant economic contributions of the defense industry create powerful incentives for state officials to protect it, using rhetoric about "job creation" and "national security" to provide political cover. This economic reliance drives military production, which in turn normalizes and facilitates the adoption of militarized technologies by domestic law enforcement, entrenching the entire system.
