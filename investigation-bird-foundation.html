Report: Academic & State Complicity

The BIRD Foundation Pipeline

This report establishes that the U.S.-Israel Binational Industrial Research and Development (BIRD) Foundation operates as a state-corporate welfare pipeline. It leverages U.S. public funds to provide risk-free subsidies to the Israeli security-industrial complex under the guise of "binational research," co-opting Arkansas's public universities in the process.

I. Deconstructing the BIRD Foundation

The BIRD Foundation presents itself as a neutral facilitator of international cooperation. However, an analysis of its structure and governance reveals it to be a state-directed instrument for subsidizing private industry, particularly the Israeli technology and security sectors, with U.S. public funds.

Governance: An Interlocking Directorate of State Power

The BIRD Foundation is directly controlled by the governments that created it. Its Board of Governors is composed of high-ranking officials from the U.S. Departments of Commerce, State, and Treasury, alongside their counterparts from the Israeli Ministries of Economy, Finance, and Innovation. This ensures that funded projects serve the strategic goals of both states.

Funding: Socialized Risk, Privatized Profit

BIRD operates on an endowment 50% funded by U.S. taxpayers. It provides "conditional grants" that cover up to 50% of a project's R&D costs. If the venture fails, the grant does not require repayment. This model socializes the financial risk of innovation onto the public while the private companies retain full ownership of their intellectual property and privatize all potential profits.

Specialized Programs: The Security-Industrial Pipeline

Specialized programs formalize BIRD's role as a funding conduit for the security state. The BIRD Cyber program, under which the Arkansas grant was awarded, is a direct partnership between the U.S. Department of Homeland Security (DHS) and the Israel National Cyber Directorate (INCD). These state security agencies jointly define the "urgent cybersecurity challenges" eligible for funding, ensuring the pipeline serves state-defined security objectives.

II. Grant Dossier: The Arkansas Connection

A comprehensive search reveals a single BIRD Foundation grant awarded to an Arkansas-based entity. This case provides a complete data set for analyzing the foundation's operational model within the state.

Bastazo / Salvador Technologies Grant

  • Program: BIRD Cyber
  • Total Project Value: \$2.2 million
  • U.S. Partner: Bastazo, LLC (Fayetteville, AR) - A startup with deep ties to the University of Arkansas system.
  • Israeli Partner: Salvador Technologies (Rehovot, Israel) - A private firm specializing in cyber-attack recovery for industrial systems.
  • Governing Agencies: U.S. Department of Homeland Security (DHS) & Israel National Cyber Directorate (INCD).

Profile: Salvador Technologies - The IDF Connection

Salvador Technologies' own marketing materials and leadership biographies repeatedly and proudly state that its core expertise is derived directly from its founders' service in the Israel Defense Forces (IDF).

Founders Alex Yevtushenko (CEO) and Oleg Vusiker (CTO) are veterans who both served for over ten years in the "National Cyber Unit and elite intelligence corps of the Israel Defense Forces (IDF)". This is a clear reference to Israel's premier signals intelligence and cyber warfare apparatus, most famously Unit 8200.

The connections are not incidental; they are the company's primary credential. The BIRD grant provides Salvador with risk-free capital, a U.S.-based partner for market entry, and the immense prestige of being selected by DHS and partnered with a U.S. university system.

III. Conclusion: Corporate Welfare & Academic Complicity

The BIRD Foundation's operation in Arkansas imposes both tangible and intangible costs. The tangible cost is a direct financial subsidy from U.S. taxpayers to a private Israeli enterprise. The intangible cost is the corrosion of the academic integrity of Arkansas's public universities, which are instrumentalized as vehicles for this subsidy.

ANALYSIS: The Subsidy Pipeline

The University of Arkansas system, through its startup Bastazo, is being used as a legitimizing vehicle. The involvement of its faculty and research provides the necessary "U.S. partner" that makes the subsidy to the Israeli firm possible. This creates a formal, publicly funded partnership between a U.S. public university system and a company whose core expertise is explicitly derived from its founders' decade-long service in the IDF's elite intelligence corps. This erodes the principle of academic integrity by subordinating the university's research mission to the commercial goals of the global security-industrial complex.
